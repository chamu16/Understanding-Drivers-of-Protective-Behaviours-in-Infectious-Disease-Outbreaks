## Introduction
During infectious disease outbreaks, controlling the spread of disease depends not only on medical interventions but also on how individuals respond through protective behaviours such as mask wearing, social distancing, and hygiene practices.

Understanding how these behaviours evolve over time is important for designing effective public health strategies. This study focuses on analysing behavioural changes dynamically by integrating behavioural, policy, and epidemiological data.



## Objective
● Analyse how protective health behaviours change over time during an outbreak.

● Identify the impact of government policies, epidemiological conditions, and individual perceptions.

● Develop a unified framework combining multiple datasets.

● Apply machine learning models to understand key drivers of behaviour.



## Results and Discussion
● Protective behaviours change dynamically over time rather than remaining constant.

● Government policies, especially stringency measures, have a strong influence on behaviour.

● Behavioural and perception-based variables (e.g., risk perception, trust in government) are key drivers.

● Epidemiological variables such as cases and deaths have relatively lower direct impact.

● Machine learning models (LightGBM and CatBoost) show strong performance for predicting certain behaviours, while some behaviours remain more complex and variable.
